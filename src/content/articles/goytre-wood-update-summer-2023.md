*This piece was written and contributed by our very own *[*Steve Wiltshire*](https://www.protect.earth/blog/introducing-steve-wiltshire)* (fondly known as Steve the Ecologist)*

Goytre Hill (pronounced Goy-tree) is almost 70 acres of rough pasture, hillside scrub and woodland in Mid-Wales that Protect Earth has recently [taken ownership of](https://www.protect.earth/blog/goytre-wood).

The views from the hilltop are spectacular with panoramic vistas of the surrounding Radnorshire hills and along the valley of the upper River Teme.

Having visited the site a few times during the winter, I was curious to see what wildlife had arrived when I visited again back in May.

## Mindful biodiversity development

Protect Earth's intention is to plant trees here – it's what we do – but it's important that tree planting doesn't affect any habitats or wildlife already present. We want more trees, but protecting and enhancing biodiversity is also the name of the game. So in order to find out what species were already using the site – it was bird survey time.

Goytre is made up of distinct habitats, not all of them suitable for tree planting. The site is accessed via a public footpath which passes through a nice area of existing broadleaf woodland. The first thing I noticed was a Rookery with 14 nests already full of noisy chicks. And as I headed steadily upwards through the trees the early morning air became full of birdsong: Wrens, Mistle Thrushes and Blackbirds, along with Redstarts, Willow and Garden Warblers fresh in from Africa. We may do some management, but no tree planting will take place in the existing woodland, however, the species present would provide evidence of what grows well here and a good clue as to what we should plant nearby. I noticed Oak, Birch, Hazel and Holly, the quintessential species of hill and valley sides in Mid Wales. I emerged from the trees and could see many hillsides up and down this wide valley were still covered with this type of woodland, but the hillside around me now was overgrazed pasture, the trees having been cleared for grazing many years ago. But evidence of historic tree cover could be seen at my feet in the form of the occasional bluebell still hanging on amongst the grass.&nbsp;

I completed a thorough lap of the site and ended up on the highest point to review my map now covered with letters and symbols indicating the bird species I'd recorded and what they were doing. Much of the site was rich in bird life, but the key area was a special habitat found on the steepest areas known in Wales as Ffridd (pronounced freeth). Here amongst the Bracken, scattered Rowan and Hawthorn were Stonechats, Yellowhammers, Tree Pipits and a pair of Whinchats – the latter two on the UK Red List as birds of the greatest conservation concern. I also noted other declining species including: Kestrel, Yellowhammer, Bullfinch, Linnet, Cuckoo (the only one I heard all spring) and a rather out of place Grasshopper Warbler (a bird more associated with wetter habitats) just passing through on migration and looking rather confused amongst the Bracken. And towering way above the rough grassland of this highest point, spilling out endless streams of song, were three Skylarks – all just tiny dots in a vast blue sky.

## Next steps for Goytre Woods

I made two other survey visits in late spring, and that data will provide the key to where we will choose to plant trees. The Ffridd will remain unplanted and a sanctuary for Whinchats and Tree Pipits; the top of the hill will also stay tree free and continue to be a good area for nesting Skylarks and Meadow Pipits. We will instead plant the grassy hillside where almost no birds were recorded. The lower parts will have Sessile Oak, Willow, Aspen, Hazel and Holly and will hopefully attract more Redstarts and Garden Warblers. Higher up we'll plant Birch, Rowan and Hawthorn to provide perches for Tree Pipits and Whinchats on the edge of the Ffridd.&nbsp;

Protect Earth have applied to the Welsh Government for a Woodland Creation Grant outlining this proposal. We hope to plant around 14,000 native trees at Goytre, while protecting and improving the valuable wildlife-rich habitat already found there. In a few years' time a mosaic of habitats will maximise the site's biodiversity, while a significant amount of new planting will be another welcome contribution to tackling the climate crisis.

## Be involved with Goytre Wood

This site and wood isn't intended to be communal for birds alone. It's a communal woodland for the people who live around it. Like any good neighbour, Protect Earth wants to ensure our Goytre Wood plans won't upset the local communities and discourage visiting. If you live near the wood and would like to know more about the project, including when we may start work and want to be a part of the conversation, please contact us.
